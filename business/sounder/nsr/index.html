History & Mission
Four breed associations merged into a single registry — the largest multi-breed purebred swine registry in America
1883
American Duroc-Jersey Swine Breeders' Association founded — red hogs from New York and New Jersey lines. First purebred swine registry in America.
1893
Hampshire and Yorkshire breed associations established. Hampshire from England via Virginia — the "belted" hog. Yorkshire from England — the "Large White" maternal line.
1950s
American Landrace Association formed as the breed arrives from Denmark. Long-bodied, drooped-ear maternal specialist joins the US registry landscape.
1994
Four breed associations merge to form the National Swine Registry, headquartered in West Lafayette, IN — adjacent to Purdue University's animal science program.
2000s
STAGES (Swine Testing And Genetic Evaluation System) becomes the standard performance testing program. EPD-based genetic evaluations published for all four breeds.
2026
Sounder partnership proposed — digital transformation of registry operations, breeder CRM, and performance analytics. The next 130 years start here.
Current Operations

The National Swine Registry maintains pedigree records, processes animal registrations, and publishes genetic evaluations for Hampshire, Duroc, Yorkshire, and Landrace breeds. Historically, Berkshire was also managed under NSR until operations were delegated to the American Berkshire Association.

NSR operates the STAGES program — the industry's primary performance testing system. Breeders submit growth rate, backfat, and loin muscle area data. STAGES computes Expected Progeny Differences (EPDs) that guide breeding decisions across the purebred sector.

Registration workflow remains largely paper-based. Breeders submit litter notifications and individual registration forms by mail or fax. Processing times can exceed 4–6 weeks. Performance data is entered manually from on-farm measurements or test station results.

Membership has declined steadily as commercial genetics companies (PIC, Topigs Norsvin, DNA Genetics) capture an increasing share of the production market. The purebred sector's survival depends on the show pig market, seedstock sales, and niche premium programs.

Four Breeds, One Platform
Each breed has distinct genetics, purpose, and market position — unified under a single registration authority
🐖
Hampshire
Terminal / Dual Purpose
Black with distinctive white belt. Known for lean carcass quality, muscling, and minimal backfat. The most popular show pig breed in America. Strong terminal sire genetics.
🐖
Duroc
Terminal
Red to golden-red. Superior growth rate, feed efficiency, and intramuscular fat (marbling). The dominant terminal sire in commercial crossbreeding programs. Largest breed by registrations.
🐖
Yorkshire
Maternal
White with erect ears. Premier maternal breed — large litter sizes, excellent mothering ability, and milk production. Foundation of most commercial dam lines in the US.
🐖
Landrace
Maternal
White with drooped ears and long body. Danish origin. Exceptional litter size and longevity. Crossed with Yorkshire to create the F1 commercial dam — the backbone of US pork production.
